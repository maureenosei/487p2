WILDFIRES

WHAT ARE WILDFIRES?

Wildfires are unplanned fires that burn in a natural area and continue to grow at an alarming rate. They can burn millions of occurs of land -- consuming everything in their paths. The speed of wildfires could be considered their most deadly trait as they can travel 14 miles per hour, easily overtaking the average human in minutes. Recently, wildfires have been one of the most common natural disasters in the United States, particularly in states like California and Oregon. Due to concerns over social distancing and respiratory dangers brought on by COVID-19, mitigation efforts have also been significantly derailed -- resulting in devastating damages.

HOW DO WILDFIRES START?

Wildfires are considered to be natural disasters, however, only 10 to 15 percent of them occur on their own in nature. The other 85 to 90 percent result from human causes, including arson, campfires, and discarded cigarettes.

Naturally occurring wildfires can spark during dry weather and droughts -- a climate common in states like California. In these conditions, green vegetation can convert into flammable fuel, strong winds can spread the fire quickly, and warm temperatures encourage combustion. With these components, the only thing missing is the spark, which can occur in many forms, including lightning, arson, a burning campfire, and a cigarette.

Three conditions must be present for a wildfire to burn: fuel, oxygen, and a heat source. Firefighters call these three elements the fire triangle, and if any of these elements are removed from the fire, it will go out.

CAN WILDFIRES BE CONTAINED?

Although wildfires are destructive and unpredictable in nature, they can be controlled and subdued. Click through the slideshow below to learn about the different measures that can be taken to control wildfires.

Backfiring

Backfiring is a tactic where firefighters intentionally set a fire along a fireline’s inner edge, in order to consume the fuel in the path of a wildfire or to change the direction it is traveling.

Air Tankers

Airtankers are fixed-wing aircrafts that can carry fire retardant or water and drop it on or in front of a fire to help slow the fire down. This method is particularly useful for extreme forest fires.

Hotshots

A hotshot crew, formally known as an interagency hotshot crew, is a handcrew of 20-22 wild-land firefighters who respond to large, high-priority fires across the country and are assigned to work the most challenging parts of the fire.

Traditional Methods

A traditional method of firefighting is simply sending out professional firefighters to stop the fire. The most common technique is to simply use water and a hose. However, with wildfires, these traditional methods are sometimes useless due to the fire's magnitude.

What can I do?

Unlike many natural disasters, most wildfires are caused by people—and can be prevented by people, too. Never leave a fire unattended. Completely extinguish the fire—by dousing it with water and stirring the ashes until cold—before sleeping or leaving the campsite. Contact 911, your local fire department, or the park service if you notice an unattended or out-of-control fire.
